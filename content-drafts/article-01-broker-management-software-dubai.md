Most Dubai developers sell the majority of their off-plan inventory through brokers. Yet at many developers, the systems behind that channel look like this: a shared spreadsheet of "registered" agencies, a WhatsApp group per project for availability, commission calculations done in Excel at month-end, and an inbox full of trade licenses waiting for someone to check them.

That setup works — right up until a launch weekend, when it very visibly doesn't.

## The four jobs a broker portal actually has

Strip away the feature lists and every broker management platform is doing four jobs. If you're evaluating software (including ours), judge it on these.

### 1. Onboarding brokers without the paperwork bottleneck

Every agency that wants to sell your inventory needs a trade license check, a RERA broker card verification, a signed agreement, and bank details for payouts. Done manually, that's days of back-and-forth per agency — and it silently caps the size of your broker network, because nobody has time to onboard the long tail.

A portal turns this into self-service: the agency registers, uploads documents, and tracks its own approval status. Your team reviews and approves against a checklist instead of chasing attachments. Document expiry tracking matters more than it sounds — broker cards lapse, and selling through an unlicensed broker is a compliance problem you find out about at the worst time.

### 2. Showing brokers live inventory they can trust

The moment a broker doubts your availability data, they stop using the portal and call your sales admin instead — and you're back to WhatsApp. Trustworthy inventory means: a single source of truth synced in near real time, unit holds with automatic expiry so nobody sits on stock, and clear rules about who sees which release.

This is also where launch-day capacity matters. A hot release can put your entire broker network on the portal in the same ten minutes. Ask any vendor what happens to unit status under that load — and ask for specifics.

### 3. Moving deals from EOI to booking in the open

An Expression of Interest scribbled in a chat message is not a pipeline. A portal gives every deal a stage — EOI, allocation, booking, SPA — with an owner, an SLA, and visibility for the broker who submitted it. Brokers stop calling for status updates because the status is in front of them.

The hard part isn't the workflow; it's the client-protection rules around it. Who "owns" a client when two brokers register the same buyer? How long does protection last? Decide these rules before the software, not during a dispute. (Our free [Broker Portal BRD Template](https://ahmedkhaire.com/resources/broker-portal-brd-template) has a whole section on conflict rules — steal it.)

### 4. Making commissions boring

Commission disputes are the fastest way to lose a broker's loyalty — and the biggest source of month-end pain for your finance team. Automated commission management means the calculation happens per deal from the agreed structure, the broker can see pending vs paid at any time, and payouts follow a published SLA with a clean audit trail. When commissions are transparent, they stop being a topic.

## Buy, build, or keep the spreadsheets?

An honest framing, since our founder [Ahmed Khaire](https://ahmedkhaire.com) spent a decade at PwC building exactly these systems for Emaar, Aldar, and Majid Al Futtaim on enterprise platforms:

- **Keep the spreadsheets** if you sell through fewer than ~10 agencies, launch rarely, and one person can hold the whole channel in their head. The overhead of any system will exceed the pain.
- **Build on your enterprise CRM** (Salesforce, Dynamics) if you're at Emaar/Aldar scale — hundreds of internal users, existing enterprise licenses, an IT department, and the budget for a 6–12 month implementation. Done right, it's powerful. It's also expensive to build and more expensive to change.
- **Buy purpose-built** if you're a mid-market developer who needs broker onboarding, live inventory, EOIs, and commissions working in weeks, not quarters. This is exactly the gap [Nogbase](https://nogbase.ae) exists to fill — the enterprise patterns, without the enterprise consulting invoice.

## Questions to ask any vendor (including us)

1. How does broker KYC handle document expiry and re-verification?
2. What is the inventory sync lag from the source of truth, worst case?
3. What happens when two brokers register the same client?
4. Can a broker see their expected commission per deal — before asking anyone?
5. What does launch-day load look like on your platform, concretely?
6. How do agencies stay isolated from each other's clients and deals?
7. What integrates with our ERP and payment gateway today, not on the roadmap?

If a vendor answers all seven without hand-waving, you're in good hands.
